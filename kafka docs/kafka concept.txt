Producer — the sender. Pushes messages into a topic. Example: your order service publishes a new order event.

  Topic — the mailbox. A named log where messages are stored. Example: orders topic holds all order events.

  Partition — a topic is split into partitions for parallelism. Think of it as lanes on a highway. orders topic with 1 partition = 1 lane. Messages
  in each partition are ordered.

  Consumer — the receiver. Reads messages from a topic partition. Example: your order-processor service reading from orders.

  Consumer Group — a team of consumers working together to process a topic. Kafka assigns each partition to exactly one consumer in the group at a
  time.

  ---
  Hierarchy:

  Producer
      └── pushes to ──► Topic
                            └── Partition 0
                            └── Partition 1
                                 └── consumed by ──► Consumer Group
                                                         └── Consumer A  (owns Partition 0)
                                                         └── Consumer B  (owns Partition 1)

  ---
  Why Consumer Groups matter:

  - If you have 1 partition and 2 consumers in the same group → only 1 consumer reads, the other idles (Kafka won't split a partition)
  - If you have 2 partitions and 2 consumers → each consumer owns 1 partition, true parallelism
  - Two different groups reading the same topic each get their own independent copy of all messages — so order-processor and audit-service can both
  consume orders without stepping on each other

  ---
  In your project: topic orders has 1 partition (partition 0), consumer group order-processor has 1 consumer reading it. When you stopped the
  consumer, the lag went to 2 — those 2 messages are sitting in partition 0 waiting.

In Kafka, every consumer must belong to a consumer group.
 Why Kafka enforces this:

  Consumer groups are how Kafka tracks offsets (how far each consumer has read). Without a group ID, Kafka has no way to remember "this consumer
  left off at message 8" — so if it crashes and restarts, it wouldn't know where to resume.

Lag exists at multiple levels:

  Partition level (most granular) — each partition has its own lag. In your case: partition 0 has lag 2.

  Consumer level — a consumer owns one or more partitions, so its lag is the sum of lag across those partitions.

  Group level — total lag across all partitions across all consumers in the group.

  ---
  In your project right now they all show the same number because you have 1 partition + 1 consumer + 1 group:

  Partition 0 lag = 2
  Consumer A lag  = 2   (owns only partition 0)
  Group lag       = 2   (only one consumer)

  With 2 partitions and 2 consumers it would look like:

  Partition 0 lag = 2  ──► Consumer A lag = 2
  Partition 1 lag = 5  ──► Consumer B lag = 5
                           ─────────────────
                           Group lag       = 7

  ---
  So when you see "total_lag": 2 in your tool result — that's the group-level lag (sum of all partition lags). The "partitions" array breaks it down
  to the partition level. Consumer-level lag is effectively the same as partition lag when each consumer owns exactly 1 partition, which is the
  common case.
